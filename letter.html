A letter from the founder

Why I'm building this

I'll keep this honest, because honesty is the whole point of porchstroll.

For years I've watched the places we go to talk to each other turn into places that talk about us instead — to advertisers, to data brokers, to whoever's buying. Somewhere along the way we became the product. Most of us know it by now. We just haven't had anywhere else to go.

I kept waiting for someone to build the alternative — something that felt like a real neighborhood instead of a slot machine. A place you could spend time without the low hum of being measured, sorted, and sold. It didn't show up. So I decided to try to build it myself.

[Personal beat — make this yours: the specific moment or experience that pushed you from "someone should build this" to "I'm going to build it." A real detail here is worth more than any mission statement.]

I made porchstroll a nonprofit on purpose. Not for a tax break — we're choosing to pay our share like anyone else who uses the same roads and schools. I did it because a nonprofit can't be sold to the highest bidder, can't be pushed by investors to start squeezing the people who use it, and can't quietly hand its profits to insiders. The promise not to exploit you isn't a setting I can flip on a Tuesday. It's built into what porchstroll legally is.

I don't think privacy should be a premium feature. I don't think a feed should be engineered to keep you angry so you'll stay a little longer. I don't think the people who build a thing should get rich while the people who use it get harvested. None of that is radical. It used to just be called being decent.

porchstroll is small right now, and I intend to keep it honest as it grows — which is why this whole site shows its work, including the parts we haven't figured out yet. If we ever stop living up to any of this, I want you to be able to point at our own words and hold us to them.

Pull up a chair. I'm glad you're here.

[Your name]

Founder, porchstroll
